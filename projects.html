Wheat Stem Sawfly Detection

The Wheat Stem Sawfly Detection Project is an applied research initiative focused on developing a portable, field-deployable NMR system capable of identifying infestations of wheat stem sawfly within standing wheat. This pest causes substantial agricultural losses by hollowing out stems during its larval stage, often going undetected until structural damage leads to lodging and yield reduction.

At the core of the system is a 0.32 Tesla, 10-pound portable Halbach array magnet, paired with a software-defined radio spectrometer (ABQMR's Soder-Spec). This combination enables sensitive magnetic resonance measurements in a compact, ruggedized platform suitable for use directly in agricultural environments. The system is designed to non-destructively probe wheat stems and detect internal biological signatures associated with sawfly larvae presence—without requiring visual inspection or plant destruction.

Unlike traditional detection methods, which rely on indirect indicators or post-damage assessment, this NMR-based approach aims to provide early, in-field detection. By capturing differences in moisture distribution, tissue structure, and biological composition within the stem, the system has the potential to distinguish infested from healthy plants in real time.

Following prototype development, the project will transition to field-based research and validation, where performance will be evaluated under real agricultural conditions. This phase will focus on signal reliability, detection accuracy, ease of use, and operational robustness across varying environmental factors such as temperature, humidity, and crop variability.

Ultimately, this project seeks to lay the groundwork for a practical diagnostic tool that empowers farmers, agronomists, and researchers to:



  • Detect infestations earlier in the growing cycle
  • Make more informed pest management decisions
  • Reduce unnecessary pesticide use
  • Minimize crop losses and economic impact


By bridging advanced magnetic resonance technology with agricultural application, the Wheat Stem Sawfly Detection Project represents a step toward precision agriculture tools that are both scientifically rigorous and operationally practical.

Major Scientific Acheivements

We pioneered the use of NMR velocity imaging in multiphase and granular flows. In particular, we obtained concentration and velocity profiles of concentrated (40-60% by volume of solids) suspensions in pipe flow as well as in couette geometry for the first time. We were the first group to measure the velocity profiles within a continuously flowing particulate flow, i.e., flows of solid particles without any liquid carrier. We have also examined the standard formulation of restricted diffusion measurements by NMR and have derived a criterion for its validity in terms of pulse sequence parameters, diffusion coefficient, and the size of the confining space. Further work has led to a derivation of an exact analytic expression for signal attenuation due to diffusion when the gradient pulse cannot be considered to be short. Finally, we have made contributions in NMR techniques and experimental methods; flow imaging schemes, Abel transforms for cylindrical geometries, coil design, and analog compensation for gradient-induced eddy current suppression.
